As AI reshapes software development, new roles emerge and existing roles evolve. Here's what's changing and what it means for teams.

## Roles in transition

### Software engineer

**Traditional focus:**

- Writing code
- Debugging
- Code review
- Technical design

**Evolving focus:**

- Orchestrating AI outputs
- Higher-level design and architecture
- Validating and refining AI output
- Human-in-the-loop oversight

**New skills needed:**

- Effective prompting
- AI output validation
- Task decomposition
- Quality judgment at scale

### Engineering manager

**Traditional focus:**

- People management
- Project coordination
- Technical guidance
- Performance management

**Evolving focus:**

- Navigating AI tool selection
- Training teams on new capabilities
- Adapting processes for AI workflows
- Managing changing skill requirements

**New skills needed:**

- Understanding AI capabilities and limits
- Change management
- Policy development
- New success metrics

### Senior / Staff engineer

**Traditional focus:**

- Technical leadership
- Architecture decisions
- Mentoring
- Complex problem solving

**Evolving focus:**

- Defining AI integration strategy
- Building AI-compatible systems
- Training others on AI use
- Quality oversight at scale

**New skills needed:**

- AI architecture patterns
- Advanced validation techniques
- Teaching AI practices
- System-level oversight

## Emerging role categories

### AI/Agent orchestrator

**What they do:**

- Design workflows involving AI agents
- Optimize team-agent interactions
- Build custom agent configurations
- Troubleshoot agent failures

**Who becomes this:**

- Engineers with strong process sense
- Those interested in tooling and productivity
- People who understand both AI and team dynamics

**Why it matters:**

- Maximizes value from AI investment
- Bridges gap between tools and workflows
- Continuous improvement of AI practices

### Prompt engineer (for development)

**What they do:**

- Develop effective prompting strategies
- Create reusable prompt templates
- Train teams on prompting techniques
- Optimize for specific use cases

**Who becomes this:**

- Engineers with strong communication skills
- Those who understand both code and language
- People who enjoy optimization and iteration

**Why it matters:**

- Consistent quality of AI interactions
- Knowledge sharing and standardization
- Continuous improvement of prompting

### AI code auditor

**What they do:**

- Specialized review of AI-generated code
- Develop AI-specific review criteria
- Track patterns in AI output
- Identify systematic issues

**Who becomes this:**

- Senior engineers with strong review skills
- Security-minded developers
- Those interested in quality assurance

**Why it matters:**

- Catch AI-specific issues
- Build institutional knowledge of AI patterns
- Quality insurance for AI adoption

### AI integration engineer

**What they do:**

- Build and maintain AI tool integrations
- Develop internal AI-powered tooling
- Optimize AI workflows technically
- Bridge AI capabilities with existing systems

**Who becomes this:**

- Engineers interested in infrastructure
- Those with platform/DevOps backgrounds
- People who enjoy tooling work

**Why it matters:**

- Smooth AI tool adoption
- Custom solutions for specific needs
- Technical foundation for AI practices

## Team structure evolution

### Small teams (< 10)

No dedicated AI roles. Everyone develops AI skills.

**What changes:**

- All engineers use AI tools
- Shared responsibility for practices
- Senior engineer leads AI practices
- Manager handles policy

### Medium teams (10-50)

Part-time AI focus roles emerge.

**What changes:**

- One or two engineers focus more on AI optimization
- Lead develops team AI practices
- May have dedicated training time
- Someone owns tool selection and management

### Large teams (50+)

Dedicated AI roles become viable.

**What changes:**

- AI orchestrator role (full or part-time)
- Specialized training programs
- AI-focused code review capacity
- Dedicated integration engineering

## Skills to develop

### For everyone

- Basic prompting competency
- Validating AI output
- Knowing when to use vs. not use AI
- Understanding AI limitations

### For leadership track

- AI tool evaluation
- Policy development
- Change management
- Team capability assessment

### For technical track

- Advanced prompting and decomposition
- AI-compatible architecture
- Custom agent development
- AI safety and security

## What this means for hiring

### Changing interview focus

- Less emphasis on syntax recall
- More emphasis on problem decomposition
- AI collaboration skills assessment
- Judgment and validation ability

### New screening questions

- How do you decide when to use AI assistance?
- Describe a time you caught an AI error
- How do you validate AI-generated code?
- What AI tools have you used and how?
